COVID-19 Dining

Helping UW students find safe, comfortable eating spaces during COVID-19 with personalized comfort preferences

University of Washington 2021 UX Research & Design
5
Contextual Inquiries
18
Survey Responses

🎯 Challenge

COVID-19 created confusing indoor eating guidelines. Students didn't know where they could safely eat on campus—crowded spaces, unclear signage, and weather forcing everyone indoors.

💡 Solution

A mobile app with personalized “Comfort Preferences” that matches students to eating spaces based on their unique needs—social distance, shelter, temperature, facilities.

📊 My Role

  • UX Researcher
  • Contextual Inquiry
  • Survey Design

Crowded Indoor Spaces
Students clustered in limited designated eating areas, unmasked while eating

Unclear COVID Guidelines
Mixed signage and unclear restrictions left students guessing where eating was allowed

“I don't want to drink inside because of the crowd. I feel more safe outside but I feel more comfortable indoors.”
— UW Student, who typically eats outside despite Seattle weather
“I just don't think it's worth it to wait for a seat. I'd rather walk 15 minutes back to my apartment.”
— UW Student, who only used their prepaid dining plan for takeout
1

Contextual Inquiry & Field Observation

Followed 5 students as they searched for eating spaces on campus. Observed their behaviors, decision-making process, and level of comfort. One participant walked 15 minutes in the rain back to his apartment rather than eat in the crowded HUB—revealing deeper safety concerns than anticipated.

Students avoided indoor eating due to unclear protocols, crowded lines, and distrust of “designated eating areas.” One student had never eaten inside the HUB despite having a dining plan. Students optimized routes for fewer crowds, kept food in coat pockets for warmth, and wore masks outside despite being with roommates.

Following Students Through Campus Observed unexpected behaviors: choosing 15-minute walks over indoor eating, avoiding crowded lines entirely, selecting shortest queues over preferred restaurants
2

Survey: What Matters Most?

Conducted a survey with 18 students to rank eating space dimensions. Top priorities: sheltered from rain, having a chair, having a table, warmth, and proximity to classes. This data informed our “Comfort Preferences” feature.

Critical insight: Students didn't know official indoor dining protocols. Many assumed “designated eating areas” were just places where crowds ate before COVID, not areas with better ventilation or safety measures—creating distrust in the system.

Data-Driven Preference Categories Survey revealed: 1) Shelter, 2) Seating, 3) Tables, 4) Warmth, 5) Proximity—“comfort” means different things to different people
3

Defining User Needs & System Gaps

Mapped the relationships between COVID restrictions, campus resources, and student needs. Identified critical gaps:

1) Information accessibility—students didn't know where eating was allowed or why. 2) No review system—students wanted crowd forecasts and compliance reports. 3) Trust issues—students questioned whether “designated” areas were actually safer.

Mapping System Gaps COVID restrictions ↔ Campus resources ↔ Students: gaps in information flow, lack of crowdsourced reviews, and trust deficit in official designations
“I always see people sitting unmasked at tables, not eating—just studying. I don't know the official protocols for when you're allowed to take your mask off indoors.”
— UW Student, who avoided all indoor dining due to unclear safety guidelines
4

Usability Testing & Evaluation

Created pre/post surveys comparing comfort levels before and after seeing the prototype. Designed 7 specific tasks: browse prototype, find restaurant pages, set up filters, locate Center Table, look up information, write reviews, and navigate using alternative methods.

Critical feedback from testing: “I like the comfort setting, it's more preferences. What means comfort to me doesn't mean comfort to other people.” This led to our breakthrough feature: personalized Comfort Preferences.

Task-Based Usability Testing Participants completed specific tasks while thinking aloud—revealed need for personalization over generic filters
Pre/Post Survey Methodology Measured changes in comfort levels, satisfaction with eating spaces, and awareness of alternative locations

Key Insight

“Comfort” is personal—some prioritize warmth, others social distance. One-size-fits-all recommendations don't work.

5

Comfort Preferences

Based on usability testing, added a personalized preference system where users customize filters for their unique definition of “comfort”: social distance, crowd level, temperature, facilities, shelter, and air circulation.

Key features added: live availability updates, operation hours, service offerings, facility details, distance from current location, shortest path directions, and a review system for continuous algorithm improvement.

Personalized Comfort Settings Users define their own comfort dimensions—algorithm matches them to spaces that fit their profile + current situation
Crowd-sourced Updates Up-to-date COVID restrictions, contact information, and the latest reviews

Design Tensions & Tradeoffs

We recognized a critical tension: raising awareness of designated eating locations could make those spaces more crowded, potentially increasing infection risk while reducing quiet study spaces on campus.

Our solution balanced three competing factors: COVID-19 restrictions, limited space availability, and user comfort needs. The Comfort Preferences system helps distribute students across spaces based on their priorities rather than funneling everyone to the same “best” location.

🔍 User-Centered Research

Contextual inquiry, field observation, surveys, and usability testing—multiple methods to validate design decisions

⚖️ Balancing Tradeoffs

Recognized unintended consequences (crowding popular spaces) and designed for distributed usage patterns

Limitations & Future Work

Time and pandemic restrictions limited our ability to conduct expert interviews on human comfort and implement real-time availability tracking technology. Future iterations would integrate IoT sensors for live capacity monitoring and partner with campus facilities for official eating zone data.

Reflection: When Research Doesn't Go As Planned

The most valuable insight came from an unexpected moment: watching a participant choose a 15-minute walk in the rain over eating indoors. I had prepared questions about table selection, sanitization habits, and mask compliance. Instead, I found myself outside, asking “why are we walking to your apartment?”

This contextual inquiry taught me that the most important artifacts aren't always the ones you expect. The rain was an artifact. The air circulation was an artifact. An empty outdoor bench became as significant as any indoor table. When the participant put his sandwich in his coat pocket for warmth, or chose a longer route to avoid crowds, these small behaviors revealed deeper patterns of avoidance and distrust that no survey could have captured.

Initially, I considered this interview a failure—we never interacted with the designated eating spaces I had planned to observe. But the participant's extreme avoidance behavior (having a dining plan yet never eating inside the HUB) represented a user segment I hadn't anticipated: students with prepaid meals and no knowledge of where they're allowed to eat, choosing to leave campus entirely rather than navigate unclear guidelines.

This experience reinforced that good research means following users where they actually go, not where you expect them to be. The solution we ultimately designed—personalized comfort preferences and crowdsourced information—emerged directly from understanding why students opted out of the system entirely.
